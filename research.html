Working Papers

  1. “Victims of their Own Success: Why the Most Effective Harm Reduction Policies are Uniquely Vulnerable to Retrenchment.” (with Aidan Connaughton and Julius Wilson)
    Abstract

    Draft available upon request.

    The literature on policy feedback devotes significant attention to policies that confer desirable resources on constituents (benefit disbursement policies), while paying relatively little attention to policies that prevent or mitigate undesirable events (harm reduction policies). First, we identify factors that shape whether policymakers turn to benefit disbursement or harm reduction approaches in addressing particular problems. We then present a theory for why successful harm reduction policies become increasingly vulnerable to challenge and reversal over time, particularly in contexts of low institutional trust. Using the case of vaccines, we provide public opinion evidence consistent with our theoretical expectations: respondents with low trust in government and low trust in expertise are more likely to have increased their skepticism of Measles, Mumps, and Rubella (MMR) vaccines and school vaccination mandates over the course of the Covid-19 pandemic. Finally, we propose two additional studies: (1) a survey-experiment to test how raising problem salience (measles) affects policy preferences and information-seeking behavior; and (2) a historical analysis comparing vaccine politics in high- and low-trust periods of the 20th century. Collectively, our work broadens the scope of policy feedback research by highlighting the unique vulnerabilities of harm reduction policies, and the importance of both problem salience and institutional trust to their long-term political viability.
